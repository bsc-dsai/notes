Introduction to Probability

Probability theory deals with the study of random phenomena. Experiments are repeatable, and each time an experiment is performed, you will get different outcomes. These outcomes often exhibit underlying patterns.

An event is a subset of outcomes from an experiment. A random experiment is one where the outcome cannot be predicted precisely. A single performance of the random experiment is called a trial.

The classical definition of probability defines the probability of an event A as:

P(A) = (Number of outcomes favorable to A) / (Total number of possible outcomes)

This definition assumes that the outcomes are equally likely.

Examples

Example 1: Selecting a Black or Red Ball

Consider a box containing 7 black balls and 3 red balls. The probability of selecting a black ball is:

P(Black) = 7/10

The probability of selecting a red ball is:

P(Red) = 3/10

These probabilities add up to 1, as per the axioms of probability.

Example 2: Rolling a Fair Die

Consider rolling a fair die. The die has six faces numbered 1 through 6. The probability of getting a 6 is:

P(6) = 1/6

Relative Frequency Definition of Probability

The next type of definition that I want to introduce to you is that of relative frequency with regards to the probability measure. It states that the probability of the event A is defined as:

P(A) = (Number of outcomes favorable to A) / (Total number of trials)

Here, N is the total number of trials. You have to perform the experiment N number of times, but N is going to be a very large number approaching infinity. With such a large number of trials, approaching infinity, we are interested in the number of possible outcomes that are favorable to event A.

Based on that, you can define P(A). One limitation in this definition is that you need to go for a large number of trials approaching infinity, which may not be feasible for many applications.

Having introduced both the classical and relative frequency definitions of probability, we notice that they work under limited circumstances. Next, we will start focusing on the axioms of probability, which we will discuss in detail later.

Example: Probability of Divisibility by a Prime Number

To demonstrate the idea of the relative frequency definition of probability, consider the following example:

The problem asks to determine the probability that a given number is divisible by a prime number.

Solution:

If you choose N integers, you know that the numbers that are divisible by a prime number, say p, will be given by n/p. Let's take p = 5 as an example. If you consider numbers from 1 to 201, you know that 5, 10, 15, and so on up to 200 are divisible by 5.

How many such numbers exist? You can calculate it as:

200/5 = 40

So, there are 40 numbers divisible by 5 (5, 10, 15, ..., 200). This means there are n/pfavorable outcomes for the event that a given number is divisible by a prime number.

In this case, we took p = 5, but in general, for a prime number p, there will be n/p possibilities. For a large value of N, approaching infinity, the relative frequency definition can be applied:

P(A) = lim n -> ∞ (n/p) / n = 1/p

In conclusion, the probability that a given number is divisible by a prime number p is 1/p.

Sample Space Definition

Having introduced the classical and relative frequency definitions of probability, we now move on to defining what is called a sample space. This concept is crucial for our study in this course.

Consider an experiment where two coins are tossed simultaneously. The outcomes of this experiment can be represented as follows:

Possible outcomes are:

  • Head,
  • Head, Tail
  • Tail, Head
  • Tail, Tail

In this case, there are four possible outcomes. These outcomes form a set, which is referred to as the sample space.

If we select the outcomes Head, Head; Head, Tail; and Tail, Head, we have a subset of the sample space. This subset represents the event that at least one head has occurred:

Subset A: {Head, Head, Head, Tail, Tail, Head}

This event describes scenarios where at least one head appears among the outcomes.

To summarize, a sample space is the collection of all possible outcomes of a random experiment. Each individual outcome in the sample space is called a sample point. The sample space can be categorized as:

  • Finite
  • Countably finite
  • Uncountable

Additionally, the sample space can be further classified into:

  • Discrete Sample Space
  • Continuous Sample Space

Categorizing Sample Spaces

Let us now continue our discussion on how to categorize the sample spaces.

1. Finite and Discrete Sample Space

Consider the scenario where you toss a fair coin. The outcomes are head and tail. These form the sample space, denoted as:

S = {Head, Tail}

The number of elements in this sample space is finite, and the outcomes are discrete.

Similarly, consider throwing a fair die. The sample space here is:

S = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}

Again, this sample space is finite and discrete. If we define an event, such as getting an even number, the event can be represented as:

E = {2, 4, 6}

Events are represented as sets. Understanding set operations is crucial, which will be covered shortly.

2. Countably Infinite Sample Space

Now, consider an experiment where you toss a fair coin until you get a head. The outcomes here are:

S = {Head, Tail Head, Tail Tail Head, Tail Tail Tail Head, ...}

In this case, the sample space is countably infinite. Although the outcomes are infinite, they can be enumerated sequentially. The outcomes are discrete but infinite in number.

3. Uncountably Infinite Sample Space

Finally, consider the scenario where you randomly pick a real number between -1 and 1. The sample space is:

S = {x | -1 ≤ x ≤ 1}

The outcomes in this case are uncountably infinite. You can pick a point with any precision (e.g., 0.50, 0.52, 0.555, ...). Therefore, the sample space is continuous because the outcomes are not discrete and are infinite in number.

Set Operations and Properties

So as I was mentioning to you, the elementary events, the sample space, they're all represented as sets. It's very important that we give a quick review of the set operations that we'll possibly consider in this course.

1. Union and Intersection

2. Properties of Set Operations

Here are some key properties:

  • A ∪ A = A: The union of A with itself is A itself.
  • A ∩ A = A: The intersection of A with itself is A.
  • A ∪ S = S: The union of A with the sample space S is S.
  • A ∩ S = A: The intersection of A with the sample space S is A.
  • A ∪ ∅ = A: The union of A with the empty set ∅ is A.
  • A ∩ ∅ = ∅: The intersection of A with the empty set ∅ is ∅.
  • A ∪ B = B ∪ A: Union is commutative.
  • A ∩ B = B ∩ A: Intersection is commutative.
  • (B ∪ C) ∪ A = (A ∪ B) ∪ C: Union is associative.
  • (B ∩ C) ∩ A = (A ∩ B) ∩ C: Intersection is associative.

3. Distributive Properties

The distributive properties are:

  • B ∩ (C ∪ A) = (B ∩ C) ∪ (B ∩ A)
  • B ∪ (C ∩ A) = (B ∪ C) ∩ (B ∪ A)

4. Complementary Properties

Complementary properties include:

  • A ∪ A' = S: The union of A and its complement A' is the sample space S.
  • A ∩ A' = ∅: The intersection of A and its complement A' is the empty set ∅.

5. De Morgan's Laws

De Morgan's Laws state:

  • (A ∩ B)̅ = A' ∪ B̅: The complement of the intersection is the union of the complements.
  • (A ∪ B)̅ = A' ∩ B̅: The complement of the union is the intersection of the complements.

The Venn diagram shows an example of De Morgan's Law. If you take A ∪ B and then complement it, you get the same region as A' ∩ B̅. Similarly, taking the complement of A ∩ B gives the same result as A' ∪ B̅.

In summary, we reviewed the basic set operations—union, intersection, and complement—along with their properties, identity properties, commutative properties, associative properties, distributive properties, and complementary properties. We also introduced De Morgan's laws.

Mutually Exclusive Sets and Partitioning

1. Mutually Exclusive Sets

So now, moving on, this is a very important concept that we will keep on using in our study. We call two sets mutually exclusive when their intersection is ∅, which is the empty set. For example, if you have two sets A and B, and they don't overlap in their elements, then they are mutually exclusive. In a Venn diagram, this means that the two sets do not overlap.

2. Example of Mutually Exclusive Sets

Consider the sets A and B where:

A is the set of even numbers: {2, 4, 6, 8, ...}

B is the set of odd numbers: {1, 3, 5, 7, ...}

The union of A and B is all positive integers: {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, ...}

The intersection of A and B is empty: A ∩ B = ∅. Hence, A and B are mutually exclusive.

3. Partitioning a Sample Space

Moving on to how to partition a sample space. A partition of the sample space is a collection of mutually exclusive subsets such that their union is the entire sample space.

4. Example of Partitioning

Consider a sample space S partitioned into N subsets: A₁, A₂, A₃, ..., Aₙ. These subsets are pairwise mutually exclusive, meaning:

Aᵢ ∩ Aⱼ = ∅ for any i ≠ j

A₁ ∩ A₂ = ∅ for i =1, j = 2

Also, their union is the entire sample space:

A₁ ∪ A₂ ∪ A₃ ∪ ... ∪ Aₙ = S

This means the sample space has been partitioned into N mutually exclusive subsets.

To summarize:

  • Mutually exclusive sets have an empty intersection: A ∩ B = ∅.
  • Partitioning involves dividing the sample space into mutually exclusive subsets whose union covers the entire sample space.

This concept of partitioning will be useful later when discussing events, total probability, and Bayes' theorem.

Axioms of probability

We discussed two definitions of probability: the classical definition and the relative frequency definition. We also categorized sample spaces into discrete and continuous types. Under discrete sample spaces, we have finite and countably infinite spaces. Continuous sample spaces contain uncountable and infinite outcomes.

We previously covered set operations such as union, intersection, and complement. We also discussed mutually exclusive events and how to partition a sample space into mutually exclusive subsets. Now, we'll focus on the foundational rules, or axioms, of probability.

1. Probability Axioms

Probability is associated with an event A. It is a number that satisfies certain axioms. The probability measure P(A) should satisfy the following three conditions:

  • 1. P(A) ≥ 0: The probability of any event A must be a non-negative number.
  • 2. P(S) = 1: The probability of the entire sample space S is 1.
  • 3. For mutually exclusive events A and B, P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) if A ∩ B = ∅.

2. Example of Mutually Exclusive Events

If A and B are mutually exclusive, meaning A ∩ B = ∅, the probability of A ∪ B is:

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B)

In this case, the two events do not overlap, so their intersection is empty.

3. Summary of Probability Axioms

To summarize:

  • Probability must be non-negative: P(A) ≥ 0.
  • The probability of the sample space: P(S) = 1.
  • For mutually exclusive events: P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) when A ∩ B = ∅.

These axioms are crucial for understanding and working with probability. They form the foundation of probability theory and are used in various applications, including deriving rules for probability, conditioning events, and more.

Complement Rule of Probability

Let's derive the complement rule for probability, which states that the probability of the complement of an event A' is given by P(A') = 1 - P(A). Consider a set A in a Venn diagram representing event A. We know that A and its complement A' together cover the entire sample space S. This is a key identity in probability theory.

1. Set Identity

The union of A and A' is equal to the entire sample space, i.e., A ∪ A' = S. The probability of the entire sample space is:

P(S) or P(Ω) = 1

2. Mutually Exclusive Events

It's important to note that A and A' are mutually exclusive, meaning they do not share any common elements, i.e., A ∩ A' = ∅. This follows from Axiom 2, which states that mutually exclusive events have a probability of:

A ∩ A' = ∅
P(A ∪ A') = P(A) + P(A')

Since A ∪ A' = S, we can substitute P(S) = 1 into the equation:

1 = P(A) + P(A')

3. Deriving the Complement Rule

By rearranging the above equation, we get the complement rule:

P(A') = 1 - P(A)

4. Example

Let's take an example where P(A) = 0.6. Then, using the complement rule, we can calculate:

P(A') = 1 - 0.6 = 0.4

If two events are equally likely, such that P(A) = 0.5, then the probability of the complement is also P(A') = 0.5.

Property: P(Φ) = 0

The next property we will derive is that the probability of the empty set, P(Φ), is equal to 0. We have already discussed this before, but let's approach it from a different angle.

1. Mutually Exclusive Events Involving Φ

Consider a set A. The intersection of A and the empty set Φ is:

A ∩ Φ = Φ

This indicates that A and Φ are mutually exclusive events. Therefore, by Axiom 3, we can express the probability of their union as:

P(A ∪ Φ) = P(A) + P(Φ)

2. Using the Union of A and Φ

Since A ∪ Φ = A, we know that:

P(A ∪ Φ) = P(A)

Substituting this into the earlier equation, we get:

P(A) = P(A) + P(Φ)

From this, we can conclude that:

P(Φ) = 0

3. Understanding the Empty Set

This result makes sense because the empty set contains no elements, meaning there are zero possible outcomes. Therefore, the probability of Φ must be 0.

4. Recap of Properties

We have now derived two key properties from the axioms of probability:

  • The complement rule: P(A') = 1 - P(A).
  • The probability of the empty set: P(Φ) = 0.

Property: P(A ∪ B) for Non-Mutually Exclusive Sets

Now, I would like to discuss the next property, which relates to the case when sets A and B are not mutually exclusive. We already know what A ∪ B is for mutually exclusive sets, as stated in Axiom 3. The condition for that was when A ∩ B = Φ, the probability of A ∪ B is simply P(A) + P(B). But what happens when A ∩ B ≠ Φ?

This is the case when A and B overlap, meaning there is an intersection between them, which we denote as A ∩ B. The question is, how do we derive the expression for P(A ∪ B) when A and B are not mutually exclusive?

To do this, let's introduce a shorthand notation to make the representation easier to follow. I will use A' ∩ B as a shorthand notation for the complement of A intersecting with B.

1. Expression for P(A ∪ B)

We can now express A ∪ B as A ∪ (A' ∩ B). This means the union of A and the complement of A intersecting with B. Visually, this covers the entire region of A ∪ B.

Now, let's examine the two regions. The first region is A, and the second region is A' ∩ B. These two regions are mutually exclusive, meaning their intersection is Φ.

Using Axiom 3, we can write:

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(A' ∩ B)

2. Further Breakdown

Next, we need to break down the expression further. We know that A' ∩ B refers to the complement of A intersecting with B. Let's now consider the event B.

From the identity property, we know that:

B ∩ S = B (where S is the sample space)

Also, we can express the sample space as A ∪ A'. Therefore, we can write:

B ∩ (A ∪ A') = (B ∩ A) ∪ (B ∩ A') = B

3. Applying Distributive Property

Now, let's visualize this on a Venn diagram. The region B ∩ A is the overlapping part of A and B, while B ∩ A' is the part of B that is outside A.

These two regions, B ∩ A and B ∩ A', are mutually exclusive, so we can write, using Axiom 3:

P(B) = P(B ∩ A) + P(B ∩ A')

We know that B ∩ A' is the same as A' ∩ B. Therefore, we can express P(A' ∩ B) as:

P(A' ∩ B) = P(B) - P(A ∩ B)

4. Final Expression

Now, substituting this back into our original expression for P(A ∪ B), we get:

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B)

This gives us the expression for the probability of the union of two events that are not mutually exclusive.

Property: P(A) ≤ P(B) if A ⊆ B

We have considered the scenario when A and B are not mutually exclusive events. We showed that P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B). Now, we will move on to the last property of probability that we can derive from the axioms.

This property states that if you have an event A where the elements of A are contained within B, or A is a subset of B, then we can show that:

P(A) ≤ P(B)

For example, consider a fair die. The sample space Ω is {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}. Let A be the event of getting a {2}, and let B be the event of getting an even number {2, 4, 6}. Here, A is clearly a subset of B, so we can say that P(A) ≤ P(B).

For A = {2}, P(A) = 1/6, and for B = {2, 4, 6}, P(B) = 3/6. Clearly, 1/6 < 3/6.

1. Proof from Axioms

We will now prove this from the axioms of probability. Suppose A is contained within B. Then:

A ∪ A' ∩ B = B

Where A'B denotes the region of B that does not include A (i.e., A' ∩ B). The union of A and A' ∩ B gives B. Also, note that A and A'B are mutually exclusive, meaning:

A ∩ A' ∩ B = Φ

2. Invoking Axiom 3

Since A and A' ∩ B are mutually exclusive, we can apply Axiom 3 to write:

P(B) = P(A) + P(A' ∩ B)

Since P(A' ∩ B) ≥ 0, it follows that P(B) ≥ P(A). This proves that if A ⊆ B, then:

P(A) ≤ P(B)

3. Recap

We have derived this important property of probability, which states that if A is a subset of B, then the probability of A is less than or equal to the probability of B. Remember the three axioms of probability as we conclude:

  • P(A) ≥ 0 (Non-negative)
  • P(Ω) = 1 (Probability of the sample space is 1)
  • If A and B are mutually exclusive, then P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B)

These axioms are fundamental in deriving all properties of probability.

Review of Probability Axioms

Welcome back. Let's review the axioms of probability once again. For any event A, we can assign a number called the probability of the event. This number, called the probability measure, must satisfy the following three conditions:

  • The number must be non-negative, meaning it must be greater than or equal to 0.
  • The probability of the whole set, defined as the sample space, must be 1.
If A and B are two mutually exclusive events (i.e., A ∩ B = Φ), then the probability of A ∪ B will be P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B).

Based on these axioms, we previously derived several important rules pertaining to probability.

1. Probability of the Complement

The probability of the complement of event A is given by:

P(A') = 1 - P(A)

2. Non-Mutually Exclusive Events

For events A and B that are not mutually exclusive (i.e., A ∩ B ≠ Φ), we have the following rule:

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B)
